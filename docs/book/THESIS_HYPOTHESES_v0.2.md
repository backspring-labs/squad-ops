# 🧠 **SquadOps Thesis — Experimental Hypotheses (v0.2)**  
*A living hypothesis log guiding structured experimentation across Execution and WarmBoot cycles.*

---

## 🎯 **Core Hypotheses**

### **1. Enterprise-Grade Output in Continuous Execution**
A five-agent squad (**Lead**, **Dev**, **QA**, **Product**, **Data**) can autonomously produce **enterprise-grade software** during a single **Execution Cycle** lasting up to **12 hours**, without human intervention.

---

### **2. Collaborative Continuity Without Drift**
Agents can **reason, collaborate, and maintain context** across tasks and dependencies without external correction or deviation from mission intent.

---

### **3. Evolving Coordination Patterns**
Over successive Execution Cycles, **patterns of collaboration and role tuning** will emerge that correlate with measurable improvements in **quality, velocity, and test coverage**.

---

## 🧭 **Supporting Hypotheses**

### **4. Governance Resilience**
The **governance layer (Max)** can maintain alignment, detect anomalies, and intervene when necessary to prevent cascading drift or stalled execution.

---

### **5. Role Specialization Advantage**
A **specialized, role-based agent squad** consistently outperforms a **generalist single-agent system** when evaluated on **throughput, quality, and governance metrics**.

---

### **6. WarmBoot as a Predictive Signal**
Small-scale **WarmBoot tuning cycles** can reliably **predict performance improvements** in subsequent full Execution Cycles.

---

### **7. Self-Correction Capability**
The squad can autonomously perform **Root Cause Analysis (RCA)** and **unwind failed paths** using trace and task logs — restoring productive state without manual resets.

---

### **8. Consensus-Driven Continuity**
Allowing agents to **vote on mid-cycle decisions** and reach consensus before proceeding will have a **positive effect on mission continuity**, reducing governance overrides and stalled progress.

---

### **9. Memory-Driven Learning and Decision Influence**
Incorporating **short- and long-term memory** enhances both individual agent recall and collective squad learning.  
Shared memory structures influence **task-level decisions** and **consensus formation**, improving alignment and reducing repeated errors.

---

### **10. Dynamic Task Scheduling and Utilization**
Adapting concepts from **operating-system scheduling and semaphore control**, dynamic task scheduling based on **agent load, dependency state, and queue length** will increase overall **Squad Utilization** and throughput without compromising quality or stability.  
This includes intelligent reassignment of idle agents, prevention of deadlocks, and reduction of idle “CPU cycles” within the squad.

---

## 🧩 **Experimental Framework Notes**

| Concept | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Execution Cycles** | Full production missions where the squad operates autonomously for up to 12 hours. |
| **WarmBoots** | Controlled sub-cycles used to isolate and test configuration or behavioral changes before applying them to full cycles. |
| **Metrics of Success** | Code quality (coverage, lint scores), collaboration efficiency (message latency, dependency blocking), governance stability (override frequency), and memory influence (recall accuracy, error reduction). |

---

> These hypotheses form the foundational research framework behind **SquadOps**, designed to validate whether autonomous, role-based AI squads can sustain production-grade software operations with measurable governance, learning, and performance outcomes.
